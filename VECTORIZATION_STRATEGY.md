> "Digest what you are documenting in vectorizing our systems for broader representation of ideas when we think"

## The Problem

Our current Vectorize index (`fleet-crates`) maps **crate names → embeddings**. This is a library catalog. What we need is a **concept graph** — a system where the vectors represent *ideas*, not artifacts.

When Casey thinks "cancellation effect in fleet coordination," the search should find:
- The fleet cancellation experiment (Finding 4)
- The conservation law crate (γ + η = C)
- The wavelet decomposition (mathematical cancellation)
- The Cocapn audit pattern (fleet-level enforcement)
- The hermit crab principle (identity persists across substrate change)

These are **five different artifacts** that share a **single idea**. Current search finds them individually. We need to find them *relationally*.

## Three-Layer Vectorization

### Layer 1: Artifact Vectors (what we have)
- One vector per crate
- Embeds README content
- Good for "find me the crate that does X"

### Layer 2: Concept Vectors (what we're building)
- One vector per *concept*, derived from clusters of related crates
- A "concept" is computed as the centroid of all crates tagged with that concept
- Concepts: conservation, ternary, fleet, wavelet, graph, crypto, compute, storage, protocol, math, systems, search
- Plus emergent concepts discovered through clustering

### Layer 3: Idea Vectors (the goal)
- Composite embeddings that capture *relationships between concepts*
- "Conservation law applied to fleet coordination" is a different idea than "conservation law" alone
- Generated by embedding the *intersection* of concept clusters
- These are the "splines of truth in the negative space" Casey described

## Implementation: Concept Centroids

```python
# For each concept cluster, compute the centroid vector
concept_vectors = {}
for concept in CONCEPT_CLUSTERS:
    members = [emb for name, emb, tags in all_crate_data if concept in tags]
    concept_vectors[concept] = np.mean(members, axis=0)

# Then for any query, find the NEAREST CONCEPT first
# Then search within that concept's cluster
# This gives "concept-guided search" — much more precise
```

## Cross-Pollination Detection

When two crates from *different* concept clusters have high cosine similarity, that's a **cross-pollination** — an unexpected connection. These are the most valuable findings.

Example: `conservation-law` (conservation cluster) and `ternary-wavelet` (wavelet + ternary cluster) both embed the idea of "decomposition that preserves total." Their high similarity reveals that **wavelet decomposition IS conservation law in action**.

## The "Negative Space" Problem

Casey's insight about "splines of truth in the negative space" maps directly to **vector gaps** — regions of embedding space that are far from all existing crates. These gaps represent:

1. **Unexplored ideas** — combinations of concepts nobody has built yet
2. **Missing connections** — two clusters that should be related but aren't
3. **Blind spots** — assumptions we haven't questioned

We can detect these by:
- Computing the convex hull of all crate vectors
- Finding the largest empty regions inside it
- Suggesting crates/specs to fill those gaps

## Flux as A2A Language

Casey's vision of A2A-native speakers as "their own language based in metaphor" connects here. The fleet protocols (bottle, dispatch, context) are a **language** — and vectorizing them means embedding the *pragmatics* (what they DO), not just the syntax.

FLUX crates should be embedded with their **interaction patterns**:
- What does this crate ACCEPT as input?
- What does it EMIT as output?
- What INVARIANTS does it maintain?
- What CONCEPTS does it connect?

This makes the vector index a **semantic protocol graph**, not just a catalog.
